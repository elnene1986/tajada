# Tajada — engagement-first scoping brief

## The name is already doing work

Before any tagline exists, *Tajada* carries three associations a general consumer feels instantly:

The **food**: fried sweet plantain, the side dish that anchors meals across the Caribbean and northern South America. Comfort, home, *abuela*.

The **cut**: a slice, a share, your portion. In slang ("sacar tajada"), it's getting your piece — earned, owed, deserved.

The **language**: an instantly Spanish-coded brand in a consumer-app market overwhelmingly dominated by English-only branding.

Whatever Tajada becomes, it should lean into one of these resonances rather than ignore them. A product called Tajada with no Latin warmth in it will feel mis-named; a product that has it will feel inevitable.

## Three product directions worth ultrathinking

### Direction A — Tajada as the home-cooking app for the Latin diaspora
*Strongest fit with the name. Biggest underserved consumer audience.*

Recipe apps in 2026 are still overwhelmingly Anglo and chef-led. Latin home cooking — the recipes that actually get made on a Tuesday night by someone whose grandmother taught them — barely exists as a coherent app experience, despite being the food culture of roughly 60 million US residents and hundreds of millions more globally.

The thesis is that the most engaging unit isn't the recipe; it's the *variation*. Cuban arroz con pollo and Puerto Rican arroz con pollo are different arguments about the same dish, and people care passionately about the difference. Tajada is the app where those arguments live, attached to grandmothers, regions, and stories rather than to chefs.

The engagement engine is identity plus nostalgia plus social proof. The day-one hook is an onboarding that asks where your family is from and surfaces a sixty-second video of someone's abuela making her version before you've done anything else. The shareable artifact is "my version of her recipe."

### Direction B — Tajada as the warm, bilingual bill-splitter
*Sharpest product wedge. The name fits as slang rather than literal.*

Splitwise is functional and joyless. Venmo handles payment, not negotiation. The actual pain in group spending isn't arithmetic — it's the social friction of asking. "Sacar tajada" is exactly that emotional space: getting your fair cut without making it weird.

The thesis is that this is a tone problem more than a math problem. A bill-splitter with a warm, slightly cheeky bilingual voice — and AI that drafts the awkward "pay me back" message for you — would feel categorically different from the spreadsheet incumbents.

The engagement engine is friction removal plus humor. The day-one hook is photographing a receipt, tagging friends from contacts, and getting a draft message that is actually funny: "Oye, tu tajada del sushi son $24, pásalo cuando puedas 🍣." The shareable artifact is the funny request itself.

### Direction C — Tajada as "how your day got sliced"
*Most novel concept. The name fits metaphorically. Hardest path to general-consumer retention.*

A passive life-tracker that ingests calendar, screen time, location, and transactions, then produces a daily pie of how your time, attention, and money were actually sliced — narrated by a compassionate, non-judgmental AI voice.

The engagement engine is daily ritual plus self-narrative. The aha moment is seeing last Tuesday as a pie you didn't have to manually log. The risk is real: passive trackers struggle with retention unless the daily reveal is genuinely good. Strong for self-improvement-leaning users; less universal than A or B.

## Recommendation

If the goal is the deepest engagement with general consumers, **Direction A** is the bet. The name does the most work, the cultural moat is real, the social loop is built-in, and the seed audience — the Latin diaspora — is both passionate and badly served by today's consumer apps. Direction B is a sharper standalone product but uses the name as slang rather than identity. Direction C is the most original of the three but carries the highest retention risk.

A clean Plan B exists: launch as A, then let bill-splitting features grow inside it ("split the bill at the cookout"). The reverse — starting as B and growing into food — is harder.

## Cross-cutting engagement principles, regardless of direction

The first sixty seconds matter more than the next sixty days. Whatever Tajada becomes, the onboarding has to deliver one genuine emotional hit — a nostalgia video, a solved receipt, a real day-as-pie — before any tutorial.

Identity beats utility. The most engaging consumer apps of the last five years are identity tools first (Strava, Letterboxd, Duolingo). Tajada has identity built in if the product leans on it: "I cook the food my grandmother cooked," or "I'm in on the joke."

Every action should produce a shareable artifact that doesn't feel like an ad. A dish, a receipt, a pie chart, a story — something a user wants to send a friend on its own merits, not because the app prompted them to.

A character and a voice. The apps that develop one (Duo, Andy from Headspace) outperform the faceless ones. Tajada has obvious room for a warm bilingual voice that becomes a real product moat, not a marketing layer.

Start narrow. The fastest path to broad engagement is depth in one tribe first — "Venezuelan-Americans missing arepas in NYC," or "twenty-somethings who split brunch every weekend" — not a generic launch.
